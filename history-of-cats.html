The Origins of Domestic Cats: Where It All Began

The story of domestic cats begins approximately 10,000 years ago in the Fertile Crescent of the Near East, in the region that is now Turkey, Syria, Iraq, and Iran. This was no accident‚Äîit coincided perfectly with the agricultural revolution, one of the most significant turning points in human history.

When humans began farming and storing grain, they unknowingly created the perfect environment for rodents. Mice and rats flourished around these early grain stores, and wild cats‚Äîspecifically the African wildcat (Felis silvestris lybica)‚Äîtook notice. These clever predators moved closer to human settlements to hunt the abundant prey, beginning a relationship that would span millennia.

Did You Know?

Unlike dogs, who were actively domesticated by humans for hunting, cats essentially domesticated themselves. They chose to live near humans because it benefited them‚Äîa relationship scientists call "self-domestication."

The earliest evidence of cat domestication comes from a 9,500-year-old grave in Cyprus, where a human was buried alongside a cat. Since cats are not native to Cyprus, this cat must have been brought to the island by boat, indicating a close bond between human and feline even in prehistoric times.

Cats in Ancient Egypt: Gods Among Mortals

No civilization in history revered cats more than ancient Egypt. By around 3000 BCE, cats had become deeply embedded in Egyptian culture, religion, and daily life. They were valued not only as pest controllers protecting valuable grain stores but as sacred beings worthy of worship.

Bastet: The Cat Goddess

The goddess Bastet (also known as Bast) was one of the most important deities in the Egyptian pantheon. Originally depicted as a fierce lioness, she evolved into a more gentle figure portrayed as a domestic cat or a woman with a cat's head. Bastet represented home, fertility, protection, and‚Äîfittingly‚Äîcats themselves.

The city of Bubastis served as the center of Bastet worship, where an annual festival drew hundreds of thousands of pilgrims. The Greek historian Herodotus described this festival as one of the largest religious celebrations in Egypt, with participants drinking, dancing, and honoring the cat goddess.

Sacred Status and Laws

Egyptian cats enjoyed an extraordinarily privileged position in society:

  • Killing a cat, even accidentally, was punishable by death
  • When a household cat died, family members would shave their eyebrows in mourning
  • Cats were mummified with the same care given to humans, with millions of cat mummies discovered in burial sites
  • Exporting cats from Egypt was strictly forbidden, though smuggling eventually spread them throughout the Mediterranean

The Cat Mummy Trade

Archaeologists have discovered cat cemeteries containing hundreds of thousands of mummified cats. Many were deliberately bred and sacrificed as offerings to Bastet. In the 19th century, so many cat mummies were found that some were shipped to England and ground up to be used as fertilizer!

Cats Across World Cultures

Ancient Rome and Greece

The Romans initially relied on ferrets and weasels for pest control, but after encountering Egyptian cats, they quickly adopted them. Roman cats spread throughout the empire, reaching Britain by the 4th century CE. The goddess Diana was sometimes associated with cats, continuing the tradition of feline divinity.

Asian Cultures

In Japan, cats arrived around 600 CE, likely from China via Buddhist monks who kept them to protect sacred manuscripts from mice. The maneki-neko (beckoning cat) figurine remains a popular good luck charm, with its raised paw thought to attract fortune and customers.

In China, cats were associated with the goddess Li Shou, who was worshipped by farmers seeking protection for their crops. The Chinese word for cat, "mao," sounds similar to the word for "80," making cats a symbol of longevity.

In Islamic culture, cats hold a special place. The Prophet Muhammad is said to have loved cats, and one hadith tells how he cut off a sleeve of his robe rather than disturb his sleeping cat, Muezza. Cats are considered clean animals and are welcome in mosques.

Norse and Celtic Traditions

In Norse mythology, the goddess Freyja rode a chariot pulled by two large cats, symbolizing fertility and love. Celtic peoples associated cats with the Otherworld and believed they could see spirits invisible to humans.

The Dark Times: Cats in Medieval Europe

The medieval period marked a dramatic and tragic shift in how cats were perceived in Europe. The Catholic Church associated cats‚Äîparticularly black cats‚Äîwith witchcraft, paganism, and the devil.

1233 CE

Pope Gregory IX's Vox in Rama

This papal decree associated cats with devil worship, leading to widespread persecution of cats throughout Europe.

1347‚Äì1351 CE

The Black Death

Ironically, the mass killing of cats may have worsened the bubonic plague by allowing rat populations to explode. The plague killed an estimated 75-200 million people.

1450‚Äì1750 CE

Witch Trials

Cats, especially black ones, were often killed alongside accused witches. Owning a cat could be used as evidence of witchcraft.

1700‚Äì1800 CE

Rehabilitation Begins

The Enlightenment brought a return to rational thinking, and cats gradually regained their status as valued companions and pest controllers.

The Modern Era: Cats Reclaim Their Throne

The 19th century marked the beginning of cats' modern era. Queen Victoria was an avid cat lover, and her affection for felines helped make cat ownership fashionable among the upper classes. The first official cat show was held at London's Crystal Palace in 1871, organized by Harrison Weir, often called the "Father of the Cat Fancy."

This era also saw the development of distinct cat breeds. While cats had existed for millennia, selective breeding for specific traits was a relatively new concept. Persian cats, Siamese, and other breeds were developed and refined during this period.

The 20th century brought cats firmly into the home as beloved pets rather than working animals. Cat food was commercially developed, veterinary care advanced, and indoor-only cat keeping became common. By the turn of the 21st century, cats had become the world's most popular pet, with an estimated 600 million domestic cats worldwide.

The History of Cat Grooming

While cats have been companions to humans for millennia, professional cat grooming is a relatively modern development. For most of history, cats groomed themselves, with humans providing only basic care.

Ancient Grooming Practices

In ancient Egypt, cats were groomed as part of their sacred status. Temple cats were bathed, oiled, and adorned with jewelry. Wealthy Egyptian families would employ servants to care for their household cats, including basic grooming.

The Victorian Era

The emergence of cat shows in the 1870s created new standards for coat presentation. Owners of Persian cats, in particular, began regular grooming routines to maintain their cats' long, luxurious coats. The first cat grooming tools were adapted from human hairbrushes and combs.

Professional Cat Grooming Emerges

Professional cat grooming as a distinct profession emerged in the late 20th century. The National Cat Groomers Institute of America (NCGI) was founded to establish standards and provide training specifically for feline groomers, recognizing that cats have unique needs very different from dogs.

The CFMG Certification

Today, the Certified Feline Master Groomer (CFMG) credential represents the highest standard in professional cat grooming. From their origins as self-appointed pest controllers in Neolithic grain stores to their current status as pampered family members with their own Instagram accounts, cats have made an incredible journey. Through it all, they've remained essentially the same: independent, mysterious, affectionate on their own terms, and utterly captivating.
